# Quiet Rift: Enigma — What is This Game?

---

## The Setting

You wake up on a moon orbiting a gas giant. The sky is wrong. The plants are wrong. And something out there is watching you.

Quiet Rift: Enigma is a first-person survival colony game set on an alien moon with its own ecosystem — predators that hunt in packs, plants that look edible but might kill you, acid rainstorms that roll in without warning, and a slowly dying colony of survivors who are counting on you to hold it together.

You're not just surviving for yourself. You're the one keeping everyone else alive.

---

## The Colony

Other survivors are out there, and you can recruit them into your camp. Once they're with you, they need food, water, sleep, and a reason to keep going. Let their morale collapse and they'll stop listening. Let it fall far enough — they leave.

Each survivor has **roles** you assign them: Gatherer, Hauler, Cook, Guard, or others. And here's the catch — **putting someone on the wrong role gives them real penalties**. A survivor who hates cooking will work slower, waste food, and be miserable about it. Forcing a natural guard into hauling logs all day tanks their morale over time. You need to actually pay attention to who does what, not just throw bodies at problems.

---

## Leadership

Every craft in your colony — Security, Engineering, Logistics, Research, Agriculture, Medical, and more — can have a **leader promoted from within your survivors**. Leaders gain experience over time and actively buff the people under them. A veteran Logistics leader keeps haulers efficient. A seasoned Security leader tightens patrol rotations and responds to threats faster.

**But craft matters.** Every survivor has a natural affinity based on what they do. A Guard is naturally suited for Security leadership. A Researcher leads Research well. If you promote your Woodcutter to run your Military — because he's the toughest person in camp — his buff to the people under him will be noticeably weaker, and the conditions he imposes when things go wrong will hit harder than they would under someone who grew up doing that job. He's not incompetent, he's just out of his element.

**Swapping leaders too often is its own punishment.** Every time you demote a leader or shuffle the role to someone else, the colony feels it. A small first swap barely registers. But keep reshuffling and you'll see instability build — morale takes a hit, work slows, and survivors start questioning who's actually in charge. This instability score decays over time as things settle, but it doesn't go away overnight.

**The most powerful leaders aren't ones you make — they're ones you find.** As you explore the world, you'll occasionally encounter survivors who are already leading something: a camp of their own, a scavenger crew, a research post. These leaders come with real experience already built in. Get a quest from a struggling Research leader, help them solve their problem, and they might agree to join your colony. Bring them back alive and you can **promote them to leader immediately** — because they've already done the job. Their buff starts higher than any freshly promoted survivor, and if you put them in a role that doesn't match their background, they adapt better than a rookie would.

---

## Day, Night, and Raids

The world runs on a real day cycle. During the day you explore, harvest, build, and manage. At night the threats come.

Raids aren't random ambushes — there's a **raid risk system** that builds based on how visible your camp is, how much noise you're making, and what's nearby. Push your luck and a raid will find you. When it hits, your survivors have to respond: guards fight, civilians take shelter or flee, and you're in the middle of it all with a weapon in your hand. Survive the night, assess the damage, rebuild.

---

## The Wildlife

The moon has its own food chain and it doesn't care about you.

Prey species like the **Ridgeback Grazer** roam in herds. Spook one and the whole herd bolts. Predators like the **Hookjaw Stalker** stalk, not charge — they circle, wait for you to be alone, then commit. The **Ironstag** can knock you flat with an antler swipe.

Wildlife can **smell you**. Carrying raw meat? Things will track you back to camp. Downwind hunting matters. Understanding which animals are dangerous and which are food is something you learn through experience — or through the Codex if you've done the research first.

---

## The Flora

The plants on this moon are not your friend by default.

Some are clearly useful — **Smokebark Trees** give you lumber, **Blackstem Reeds** grow near water and have multiple uses. But others are a gamble. **Rustcap Fungus**, for example, looks like food. There's a safe variety and a toxic variety, and they look nearly identical until you know what to look for. Eat the wrong one without having researched it and you will find out the hard way.

**You can't just look at something and know if it's safe.** You have to discover it through the Codex — which unlocks the more you interact with, research, and study a species.

---

## Research

Back at camp, your **Research component** lets you build out a **tech tree** over time. Not all research is done sitting at a table — some of it is micro-research that builds up passively as your survivors work, explore, and encounter things in the world.

Research unlocks better tools, new crafting recipes, knowledge about species, improved station efficiency, and more. You'll want to keep pushing it forward because the moon has secrets, and most of them are locked behind understanding.

---

## Crafting and Stations

Your camp is built around **crafting stations**: a Workbench for tools and gear, a LogYard that processes timber, a Pantry for cooking, an AnvilForge for metal work (needs a trained engineer), and a Generator that powers the more advanced stations.

Stations don't run themselves — **NPCs work them**, and their skill and role fit determines how well they work. Automated haulers move materials between depots and stations so the workflow keeps running even when you're out exploring. Building an efficient supply chain is its own puzzle.

---

## Quests

There's a **main quest line** that drives the story forward — why are you here, what happened, what is this place actually hiding. Missions unlock in sequence and each one peels back another layer.

Alongside the main story are dynamic tasks that emerge from your colony's situation: a survivor is sick and needs specific herbs, a raider faction is moving closer and you need to push them back, a herd of wildlife is disrupting your gathering routes. These aren't always marked on a map. Some you have to notice.

---

## Exploration

The world is procedurally generated around a seed, meaning every run has the same moon but placed differently. **Points of interest** — ruins, anomalies, old camp remnants — are scattered across biomes. Some hold supplies. Some hold lore. Some hold things you probably shouldn't have found.

The more you explore, the more your Codex fills in. And the more your Codex fills in, the better equipped you are to survive what's out there.

---

## The Vibe

This isn't a power fantasy. You will lose survivors. You will make a bad call on a leader and watch morale crater. You will eat a mushroom you shouldn't have. You will be out at night, alone, low on ammo, with something circling you that you can't quite see.

But when the colony is humming — food coming in, planks getting processed, a survivor you promoted to Guard leader holding the line through a night raid — it feels like something you actually built. Because you did.
